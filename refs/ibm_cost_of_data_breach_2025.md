# IBM Cost of a Data Breach Report 2025
## The AI Oversight Gap

> **Source:** IBM Security / Ponemon Institute — *Cost of a Data Breach Report 2025*
> **Coverage period:** March 2024 – February 2025
> **Sample:** 600 organizations, 16 countries/regions, 17 industries, 3,470 interviews
> **Full citation:** IBM Corporation & Ponemon Institute LLC, *Cost of a Data Breach Report 2025*, 2025.
> **Report URL:** https://www.ibm.com/reports/data-breach

---

## Executive Summary

This edition marks 20 years of IBM/Ponemon data breach research and is subtitled **"The AI Oversight Gap."** The central finding is that AI adoption is outpacing security and governance. Organizations that deploy AI without proper controls are more likely to be breached — and those breaches cost more.

### Headline Numbers at a Glance

| Metric | 2025 Value | Change vs 2024 |
|--------|-----------|----------------|
| Global average cost of a data breach | **USD 4.44M** | -9% (first decline in 5 years) |
| US average cost (record high) | **USD 10.22M** | +9% |
| Added cost — high shadow AI vs. low/none | **USD 670K** | New metric |
| Added cost — shadow AI incident | **USD 200K** | New metric |
| Cost savings — extensive security AI & automation | **USD 1.9M** | +USD 200K vs 2024 |
| Avg cost — organizations using extensive security AI/automation | **USD 3.62M** | — |
| Avg cost — organizations using no security AI/automation | **USD 5.52M** | — |
| Avg cost — malicious insider attack | **USD 4.92M** | Highest among vectors |
| Healthcare avg breach cost | **USD 7.42M** | Highest industry, 12th consecutive year |

> According to IBM/Ponemon (2025), the global average cost of a data breach fell for the first time in five years, driven primarily by faster AI-powered detection and containment, while simultaneously reaching a record high in the United States at USD 10.22 million.

## 4. Data Breach Lifecycle

### Mean Time to Identify (MTTI) and Contain (MTTC)

| Year | MTTI (days) | MTTC (days) | Total |
|------|------------|------------|-------|
| 2017 | 191 | 66 | 257 |
| 2018 | 197 | 69 | 266 |
| 2019 | 206 | 73 | 279 |
| 2020 | 207 | 73 | 280 |
| 2021 | 212 | 75 | 287 |
| 2022 | 207 | 70 | 277 |
| 2023 | 204 | 73 | 277 |
| 2024 | 194 | 64 | 258 |
| 2025 | 181 | 60 | **241** |

2025 marks a **nine-year low** in breach lifecycle duration. This improvement is attributed to AI-driven and automation-driven detection and response.

### Breach Cost by Lifecycle Length (2025, USD millions)

| Lifecycle | 2025 | 2024 |
|-----------|------|------|
| < 200 days | 3.87 | 4.07 |
| > 200 days | 5.46 | 5.01 |

Containing a breach under 200 days saves approximately **USD 1.59 million** compared to breaches that take longer.

### Breach Identification by Source (2025)

| Identifier | % of Breaches | Avg Cost (USD M) |
|-----------|--------------|-----------------|
| Organization's security teams and tools | **50%** | $4.18 |
| Benign third party | 31% | $4.43 |
| Disclosure from the attacker | 19% | $5.08 |

Internal detection grew from 42% in 2024 to 50% in 2025. Internally detected breaches cost USD 4.18M vs. USD 5.08M when disclosed by the attacker.

> IBM/Ponemon (2025) found that 97% of organizations that experienced an AI-related breach had lacked proper AI access controls, with the most common incidents occurring through the AI supply chain via compromised apps, APIs, or plug-ins.

## 8. Shadow AI

Shadow AI is defined as the use of AI without employer approval or oversight.

### Prevalence

- **20%** of organizations experienced a security incident involving shadow AI.
- **11%** were unsure whether they had.
- **69%** reported no shadow AI incident.

Shadow AI incidents accounted for **20% of all breaches** — 7 percentage points higher than sanctioned AI incidents (13%).

### Shadow AI Cost Premium

- Breaches with **high shadow AI** cost USD 4.74M vs. USD 4.07M for low/no shadow AI — a premium of **USD 670K**.
- Shadow AI breaches added approximately **USD 200K** to the global average breach cost.
- Detection and containment time for shadow AI breaches: **247 days** vs. 241 days globally.

### Data Compromised in Shadow AI Incidents

| Data Type | Shadow AI % | Global % |
|-----------|------------|---------|
| Customer PII | 65% | 53% |
| Intellectual property | 40% | 33% |
| Employee PII | 34% | 37% |
| Other corporate data | 31% | 34% |

Customer PII is disproportionately compromised in shadow AI incidents (65% vs. 53% globally).

## 9. AI Governance

### Governance Adoption Gap

- **63%** of organizations **lacked AI governance policies** to manage or detect shadow AI.
- **37%** had AI governance policies in place.
- Among organizations with governance policies, only **34%** performed regular audits for unsanctioned AI.
- **87%** of organizations reported having no governance policies or processes to mitigate AI risk.
- **>75%** reported not performing regular adversarial testing on their AI models.

> According to IBM/Ponemon (2025), most breached organizations either don't have an AI governance policy or are still developing one, and only a minority of those that do have policies perform regular audits for unsanctioned AI.

### AI Governance Policy Components (among the 37% with policies)

| Use of AI governance frameworks | 39% |

## 10. AI-Driven Attacks

- **16%** of breaches involved attackers using AI.
- Attackers primarily used AI for human manipulation:
  - **AI-generated phishing or other communications:** 37% of AI-driven attacks
  - **AI deepfake attacks:** 35% of AI-driven attacks
- IBM research previously found AI reduced the time to craft a convincing phishing email from **16 hours to 5 minutes**.

> The IBM/Ponemon (2025) report found that 16% of data breaches involved AI-driven attack techniques, predominantly AI-generated phishing (37%) and deepfake impersonation attacks (35%).

---

## 11. Ransomware Attacks

- **63%** of ransomware victims refused to pay the ransom (up from 59% in 2024).
- Average cost of an extortion/ransomware incident remained high, especially when disclosed by the attacker (USD 5.08M).
- **40%** of ransomware victims involved law enforcement (down from 52% in 2024).
- Organizations that involved law enforcement saved approximately **USD 1 million** on average.

## 16. Recommendations (IBM Expert Guidance)

IBM security experts identified five key recommendations for reducing breach costs and governing AI:

### 1. Fortify Identities — Human and Machine
- Implement AI-powered IAM across the organization.
- Extend the same identity rigor to **non-human identities (NHIs)** such as AI agents.
- Adopt **phishing-resistant authentication methods** (passkeys, MFA).
- Manage and rotate credentials; distinguish managed (vaulted) from unmanaged credentials.

### 2. Elevate AI Data Security Practices
- Implement data discovery and classification.
- Apply access control, encryption, and key management to AI data stores.
- Use AI security services to maintain data integrity and avoid compromise.
- Data is the fuel for AI — it is a prime target for attackers.

### 3. Connect Security for AI and Governance for AI
- Treat "security for AI" and "governance for AI" as complementary, not siloed disciplines.
- Ensure CISOs, CROs, and CCOs collaborate and invest in integrated security and governance software.
- Capabilities to invest in:
  - Gain visibility into all AI deployments
  - Identify and mitigate vulnerabilities
  - Protect prompts and data generated from unintended use
  - Use observability tools to detect anomalies and improve compliance

### 4. Use AI Security Tools and Automation to Move Faster
- Security AI tools can reduce alert volumes, identify at-risk data, detect in-progress breaches, and enable faster, more precise response.
- Counter AI-powered attacker tools with equivalent defensive AI capability.

### 5. Improve Resilience
- Build capabilities to detect breaches quickly, contain them, and recover operations with minimal disruption.
- Test IR plans regularly; train both technical and non-technical leaders.
- Maintain backup and restoration protocols.
- Participate in cyber range crisis simulation exercises.

## 17. Research Methodology

- **Method:** Activity-based costing (not a statistical sample)
- **Study period:** March 2024 – February 2025
- **Organizations studied:** 600 (16 countries, 17 industries)
- **Interviews:** 3,470 technology, security, and C-suite leaders
- **Breach size range studied:** 2,960 to 113,620 compromised records
- **Cumulative research:** 6,485 organizations studied since 2005

**Cost categories measured:**
1. Detection and escalation (forensics, assessment, crisis management, board communications)
2. Notification (regulatory disclosure, communications to affected parties)
3. Post-breach response (credit monitoring, legal, regulatory fines, victim services)
4. Lost business (revenue loss, customer churn, operational downtime, reputational damage)

**Limitations noted by researchers:**
- Non-statistical, judgmental sample (not generalizable via confidence intervals)
- Sampling frame biased toward organizations with mature security programs
- Nonresponse bias not tested
- Currency conversions use local currencies at Federal Reserve rates as of 1 March 2025
